problem: "Okamed was spending ~$50K/yr on fragmented third-party platforms that still didn't support how their teams actually worked. Technicians were locked to desktop tools in hospital environments that demanded mobile. Managers had no real-time visibility into field operations. Leadership had no reliable SLA or productivity reporting. The engagement started as a website redesign — but field research revealed the real problem was the entire operational architecture."
approach: "Ran field research with technicians, managers, and ops teams to map how work actually happened — not how leadership assumed it did. What surfaced was a layer of hidden inefficiencies nobody had fully mapped: desktop-dependent workflows, fragmented communication, manual processes, near-zero field visibility. Redefined the brief entirely. Working alongside a single developer and using AI as a methodology throughout — accelerating design, prototyping, documentation, and frontend implementation — built Argos in 3 months. The platform replaced every third-party tool: mobile-first workflows with offline access for hospitals with unstable connectivity, service order management, equipment tracking, real-time dashboards, and SLA reporting."
outcome: "Okamed expanded from 210 to 333 active clients without growing the team. Now operating across 10 Brazilian states with LATAM expansion underway. ~$50K/yr in fragmented tools replaced by a single platform. Two people, three months, full delivery."
tradeoffs: "The original scope was a website redesign — a fraction of what was actually built. Redefining the brief mid-engagement meant pushing back on the client's initial framing and earning trust before expanding scope. The decision to use AI as a core methodology (not a finishing tool) was a deliberate bet: it compressed a 6-month project into 3, but required a developer willing to pair closely on implementation rather than receive traditional handoffs."
